Before the gates of Jurassic Park ever opened to the public, prospective visitors could already imagine their journey across Isla Nublar thanks to the beautifully illustrated 1993 park map and visitor guide. Distributed as a promotional brochure during the park's final preparation stages, this guide offered a glimpse into what would have been the world's most ambitious wildlife attraction.

The brochure presents Jurassic Park as a fully operational destination, complete with guided tours, visitor facilities, transportation links, and dinosaur exhibits spread across the island. Looking back today, it serves as a fascinating snapshot of a theme park that never opened in its original form.

## A Tour of Isla Nublar

The map depicts the entirety of Isla Nublar, located approximately 120 miles west of Costa Rica. A network of roads winds through the island's dinosaur paddocks, allowing visitors to experience prehistoric creatures from the safety of automated tour vehicles.

At the heart of the park sits the iconic Visitor Centre, instantly recognisable by its vast circular thatched roof and grand entrance. This building was intended to be the starting point for every guest experience, housing exhibits, restaurants, educational displays, and viewing areas overlooking parts of the park.

The tour route would have taken visitors past a variety of dinosaur enclosures, including:

- Brachiosaurus
- Gallimimus
- Triceratops
- Parasaurolophus
- Proceratosaurus
- Metriacanthosaurus
- Segisaurus
- Tyrannosaurus rex
- Velociraptor
- Dilophosaurus
- Herrerasaurus
- Baryonyx


The map also highlights the island's extensive electric fence system, which separated the various paddocks and formed the backbone of Jurassic Park's safety infrastructure.

## The Famous Ford Explorer Tour

One of the most memorable elements featured in the brochure is the automated Ford Explorer tour system. Visitors would board specially modified vehicles painted in Jurassic Park's distinctive green, yellow, and red livery before embarking on a guided journey around the island.

Unlike traditional safari parks, these vehicles were designed to operate automatically along a dedicated track, allowing guests to enjoy a narrated tour while the park's sophisticated computer systems handled navigation and safety.

The Explorer tour became synonymous with Jurassic Park and remains one of the most recognisable fictional ride systems ever conceived.

## Visitor Facilities

Despite its wilderness setting, Jurassic Park was planned as a fully-fledged resort destination. The map identifies several key facilities:

- Visitor Centre
- Port
- Heliport
- Access Roads
- Automated Tour Roads


Guests arriving by sea or helicopter would be transported to the Visitor Centre before beginning their exploration of the island.

## Kodak Sponsorship

The visitor guide also reveals one of Jurassic Park's corporate partnerships. Kodak Gold Plus film was promoted throughout the brochure as the preferred way to capture memories of the park's dinosaurs and spectacular scenery.

The advertisement encourages guests to "Catch the True Colors of Jurassic Park," reflecting the widespread corporate sponsorship arrangements that were becoming common at major attractions during the early 1990s.

## A Park That Never Opened

While the brochure paints the picture of a completed attraction ready to welcome visitors, Jurassic Park never opened in the form shown on this map. A series of catastrophic incidents during the park's final testing period exposed fundamental flaws in the park's security and animal containment systems.

As a result, the planned grand opening was abandoned, and Isla Nublar remained closed to the public.

For years, the island stood as a monument to one of the most ambitious entertainment projects ever attempted. The 1993 map therefore represents a fascinating "what might have been"—a detailed vision of a revolutionary wildlife park that existed only on paper.

## The Legacy Lives On

Although Jurassic Park itself never opened, the dream eventually returned decades later in the form of Jurassic World. Built on the foundations of the original concept, Jurassic World succeeded where its predecessor failed by finally welcoming visitors to Isla Nublar and realising much of John Hammond's vision for a dinosaur theme park.

Today, the 1993 Jurassic Park map remains one of the most sought-after pieces of fictional theme park ephemera. Its colourful artwork, detailed layout, and glimpse into an unrealised attraction make it a perfect example of why historic theme park maps continue to fascinate enthusiasts around the world.

For theme park fans, few maps tell a more compelling story than Jurassic Park's original 1993 visitor guide—a souvenir from a park that was always destined to become legendary.
